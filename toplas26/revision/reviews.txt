> Comments to the Author
> The article presents work on domain-specific language family for
> proving security-relevant hyperproperties -- specifically, real/ideal
> relationships for establishing secure multi-party computation.  The
> base language, Overture, is a small sequential language for describing
> cryptographic protocols, with explicit syntax for describing which
> participant ("client") executes a computation or accesses state.
> Computations are here over general finite fields, in contrast to
> previous work that only considered the field F_2.
> 
> Extending work presented in the ESOP paper, the present article
> contributes a Hoare logic for Overture that not only applies to
> general fields but is compositional as functions are given explicit
> specifications; in fact the existence of a frame rule also provides an
> aspect of (client-)local reasoning. The logic is rather
> straightforward, resting on the fact that assertions can directly
> reflect the code and on conventions like the one that disallows
> assigning to variables repeatedly.  Then, a type system is presented
> which supports reasoning about cryptographic shares, by introducing
> types that model visibility aspects ("public") and terms model
> operations on shares (splitting, reconstruction, ..). Complementing
> this confidentiality-based type system, a taint analysis is then
> presented for addressing integrity.
> 
> To facilitate more abstract protocol modeling, an extension of
> Overture, Prelude is defined, with a logic and type systems that arise
> by lifting the earlier formalisms. Example derivations illustrate the
> capabilities and lead up to an evaluation on actual MPC protocols,
> with experimental results.
> 
> Strengths:
> -------------
> 
> - facilitates automatic verification of multiple security properties
>  under different attacker models in a uniform language-based
>  perspective
> 
> - the development appears technically solid, and is clearly described
> 
> - the theoretical work on designing the reasoning formalisms is
>  underpinned with a practical implementation, and concrete examples.
> 
> Weaknesses/limitations:
> --------------------------------
> 
> - the article could be more explicit in describing any insight that
>  allows the authors to move from F_2 to general fields; at present,
>  it seems this is only due to improved SMT libraries.
> 
> - the differences the ESOP paper appears more substantial than is
>  typically the case, but this is mostly discussed in the
>  introduction; more specific comparisons in the technical parts would
>  have been appreciated
> 
> - the discussion of related work appears cursory; future work is not discussed
> 
> - a limitation of the present language appears to be that clients
> appear to be effectively hard-coded, preventing protocols that are
> defined over an unbounded (or unknown) number of participants.
> 
> Overall, the article presents technically sound work. Once the points
> below are addressed and discussions of limitations/future work
> etc. are provided, I recommend that it be accepted.
>

> Referee: 2

> Comments to the Author
> Summary:
> 
> The paper presents a framework for compositionally verifying security properties of MPC protocols written in the Prelude/Overture language. As suggested by the title, the key technique is an integration of constraint predicates into the security typing rules, which allows the actual implementation to use SMT solving. Compared to the conference version of this work, the main advancement is a Share type system that allows more nuanced confidentiality analysis of secret sharing protocols.
> 
> --------------
> Overall Thoughts:
> 
> Generally speaking, I think the technical contributions are worth accepting, but the presentation is rough. The submission does not stand on its own, with many references to things being explained in [29], [30], or just being "standard" and needing no explanation. This is despite the framework being quite complex with many pieces (Overture syntax and semantics, adversarial semantics, Overture SMT-based Hoare logic, Overture Hoare logic, share types, integrity types. Rinse and repeat for Prelude syntax and semantics, abstract Hoare logic, share type checking), where often it's not quite clear how the pieces fit together to build up to the desired goal. It sometimes reads like a long appendix or tech report, with some sections being a series of the definitions stitched together, and the typing and Hoare logic rules seldomly being fully explained.
>

> Comments to the Author
> Summary
> ------------
> This paper presents a framework for automated verification of low-level MPC protocols by combining: a low-level protocol language (Overture), a higher-level specification language (Prelude), SMT-based Hoare logic, and type systems for confidentiality and integrity.
> 
> Overture provides a formal model of MPC protocols with probabilistic and adversarial semantics.
> Prelude is a high-level programming and verification layer built on top of Overture for specifying MPC computations. It supports compositional reasoning via a Hoare logic and integrates with SMT-backed verification to ensure correctness and security properties. The key goal is to bridge the gap between high-level program specifications, and
> low-level cryptographic protocols
> 
> Strengths
> ------------
> 
> + A major strength is the clean separation between Prelude and Overture: Prelude expresses what to compute whereas Overture expresses how to compute securely.
> 
> + Prelude introduces a Hoare logic for high-level MPC programs
> 
> + The integration of Prelude with SMT-based constraint reasoning through Overture
> 
> + A key strength is that the system decomposes verification into orthogonal layers: correctness layer using Prelude Hoare logic, confidentiality layer using share types, integrity layer using integrity types and assertions; and hyperproperties. Together, imply security.
> 
> Weaknesses
